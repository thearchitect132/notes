GLIRL Continuity Visualisation (v0.1)

Visual Models of Identity, Narrative, and Structural Continuity



1\. Purpose of Continuity Visualisation

Continuity Visualisation provides the civilisation with a visual language for understanding:



identity evolution



figment constellations



action derivation



narrative arcs



invariant preservation



cross‑layer alignment



continuity breaks



continuity repair



It answers:



What does continuity look like?



Visualisation is not decoration — it is a cognitive instrument.



2\. Visual Grammar of Continuity

Continuity visualisation uses a consistent grammar:



Nodes represent structural states



Edges represent continuity relations



Layers represent Core → Mid → Deep → Envelope



Colours represent continuity status



Shapes represent vertex type



Patterns represent invariant state



Overlays represent narrative arcs



This grammar ensures that continuity is readable at a glance.



3\. Node Types

Each node type has a distinct visual form.



3.1 Identity Nodes

Shape: hexagon  

Meaning: stable identity state

Colour: blue (stable), yellow (transforming), red (broken)



3.2 Figment Nodes

Shape: diamond  

Meaning: potential future

Colour: green (possible), grey (pruned), orange (in tension)



3.3 Action Nodes

Shape: square  

Meaning: realised event

Colour: white (neutral), blue (aligned), red (invalid)



3.4 Narrative Nodes

Shape: circle  

Meaning: symbolic position

Colour: purple (resonant), black (collapsed)



3.5 Invariant Nodes

Shape: triangle  

Meaning: invariant state

Colour: teal (preserved), red (violated)



This creates a visual ontology of continuity.



4\. Edge Types

Edges encode continuity relations.



4.1 Identity Edges

Style: solid line  

Meaning: identity continuity



4.2 Figment Edges

Style: dashed line  

Meaning: figment evolution



4.3 Action Edges

Style: double line  

Meaning: figment collapse → action



4.4 Narrative Edges

Style: curved line  

Meaning: narrative arc



4.5 Invariant Edges

Style: dotted line  

Meaning: invariant preservation



4.6 Cross‑Layer Edges

Style: gradient line  

Meaning: alignment across layers



Edges are typed, directional, and colour‑coded.



5\. Layer Visualisation

Continuity is visualised across four horizontal layers:



Core Layer — physical invariants



Mid Layer — coordination and roles



Deep Layer — cognition and simulation



Envelope Layer — narrative and meaning



Each layer is a band in the visualisation.



Cross‑layer edges show alignment or misalignment.



6\. Continuity Map Visualisation

A Continuity Map is visualised as:



a multi‑layer DAG



with typed nodes



with typed edges



with narrative overlays



with invariant markers



This allows the viewer to see:



identity lineage



figment constellation



action derivation



narrative arcs



invariant preservation



cross‑layer coherence



…all at once.



7\. Continuity Graph Visualisation

Continuity Graphs are visualised as:



force‑directed graphs for figments



linear DAGs for identity



tree‑like structures for narrative arcs



constraint networks for invariants



bipartite graphs for cross‑layer alignment



Each subgraph is visually distinct but integrated.



8\. Visualising Continuity Breaks

Continuity breaks appear as:



broken edges



red nodes



crossed‑out invariants



orphaned figments



misaligned layers



collapsed narrative arcs



The visualisation makes breaks immediately obvious.



9\. Visualising Continuity Repair

Repair is visualised as:



ghost nodes (reconstructed lineage)



bridge edges (inserted arcs)



rebound edges (restored alignment)



patched invariants (repaired constraints)



re‑contextualised narrative arcs



Repair is shown as a healing animation in the graph.
